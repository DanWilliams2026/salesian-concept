Saint John Bosco

Don Bosco

Priest, educator, visionary

Saint John Bosco — known affectionately as "Don Bosco" — was born in 1815 near Turin, Italy, the son of a peasant farmer. He knew poverty intimately, and that experience shaped his life's work: a passionate commitment to the care and education of young people, particularly those most at risk of falling through the cracks of society.

Ordained a priest in 1841, Don Bosco began gathering young men from the streets of Turin, offering them not just religious instruction but practical education, vocational training, and above all — a sense of belonging and worth. His work grew rapidly, and in 1859 he founded the Society of Saint Francis de Sales (the Salesians), named after the gentle saint whose approach to pastoral care inspired him.

By the time of his death in 1888, the Salesian Order had established schools, workshops, and oratories across Europe and South America. He was canonised by Pope Pius XI in 1934 and is venerated as the patron saint of youth and young workers.

The Preventive System

Don Bosco's educational vision

Don Bosco's approach to education — the Preventive System — stands in contrast to the punitive methods common in 19th-century schools. Rather than waiting for bad behaviour and then punishing it, the Preventive System surrounds young people with care, reason, and religion so that they never feel the need to transgress.

R

Reason

Don Bosco believed that young people respond to being treated with respect and intelligence. Rules are explained, not merely imposed. Trust is built through transparency and dialogue.

R

Religion

For Don Bosco, faith was not an external discipline but an internal compass. He wanted young people to understand and embrace the values that guide a good life.

L

Loving Kindness

Amorevolezza — the Italian word Don Bosco used — means more than mere affection. It is an active, attentive love that notices each young person's unique gifts and struggles.

His legacy

The Salesians worldwide

Today the Salesian Order operates in over 130 countries, serving more than 1 million young people through schools, oratories, vocational training centres, and parish communities.
